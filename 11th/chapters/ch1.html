Chapter 1 — Some Basic Concepts of Chemistry

1.1 Importance of Chemistry

Chemistry plays a central role in science and is often intertwined with other branches of science. It deals with the composition, structure, properties, and interactions of matter. Almost every aspect of our daily life involves chemistry — from the food we eat to the medicines we take.

1.2 Nature of Matter

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. Matter can exist in three physical states — solid, liquid, and gas. These states can be interconverted by changing the conditions of temperature and pressure.

Classification of Matter

At the macroscopic level, matter can be classified as mixtures or pure substances. Pure substances can be further classified as elements or compounds. Mixtures can be homogeneous or heterogeneous.

1.3 Properties of Matter and Their Measurement

Every substance has unique or characteristic properties that can be classified into two categories — physical properties and chemical properties. Physical properties are those which can be measured or observed without changing the identity of the substance. Chemical properties require a chemical change to occur.

1.4 Uncertainty in Measurement

Many times in calculations, we have to deal with experimental data obtained through measurement. There are meaningful ways to handle the numbers — through significant figures and scientific notation. Every measurement has some degree of uncertainty associated with it.

1.5 Laws of Chemical Combination

The laws of chemical combination describe the fundamental principles governing how elements combine to form compounds. These include the Law of Conservation of Mass, Law of Definite Proportions, Law of Multiple Proportions, Gay Lussac's Law of Gaseous Volumes, and Avogadro's Law.

1.6 Dalton's Atomic Theory

John Dalton proposed the atomic theory of matter, which provided a rational explanation for the laws of chemical combination. According to this theory, all matter is made up of atoms which are indivisible and indestructible. All atoms of a given element are identical in mass and properties.

1.7 Atomic and Molecular Masses

The atomic mass of an element is the average relative mass of its atoms compared to an atom of carbon-12. One atomic mass unit (amu or u) is defined as exactly 1/12th the mass of one carbon-12 atom. Molecular mass is the sum of atomic masses of all atoms in a molecule.

1.8 Mole Concept and Molar Masses

The mole is the amount of substance that contains as many entities as there are atoms in exactly 12 g of carbon-12. This number is called Avogadro's number (Nₐ = 6.022 × 10²³). The mass of one mole of a substance in grams is called its molar mass.

1.9 Stoichiometry and Stoichiometric Calculations

Stoichiometry deals with the calculation of masses and volumes of reactants and products involved in a chemical reaction. It is based on the law of conservation of mass and the concept of the mole. Limiting reagent is the reactant that gets consumed first and determines the amount of product formed.
