Structure of Atom

2.1 Discovery of Subatomic Particles

Atoms are composed of three fundamental particles: electrons, protons, and neutrons. The discovery of these particles revolutionized our understanding of atomic structure.

Electron (e⁻): Discovered by J.J. Thomson in 1897 through cathode ray experiments. It carries a negative charge of 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C and has a mass of 9.109 × 10⁻³¹ kg.

Proton (p⁺): Discovered by Ernest Rutherford through gold foil experiment. It carries a positive charge equal in magnitude to electron and has a mass of 1.673 × 10⁻²⁷ kg.

Neutron (n⁰): Discovered by James Chadwick in 1932. It is electrically neutral and has a mass of 1.675 × 10⁻²⁷ kg.

2.2 Thomson Model of Atom

J.J. Thomson proposed the Plum Pudding Model in which the atom was visualized as a positively charged sphere with electrons embedded in it like plums in a pudding.

2.3 Rutherford's Nuclear Model

Rutherford's gold foil experiment led to the discovery of the nucleus. His model proposed:

• Most of the mass and all positive charge is concentrated in a tiny nucleus
• Electrons revolve around the nucleus in circular paths
• Most of the atom is empty space
• The size of nucleus is about 10⁻¹⁵ m while atom is about 10⁻¹⁰ m

2.4 Bohr's Model of Atom

Neils Bohr proposed a model based on quantum theory. The main postulates are:

• Electrons revolve in fixed circular paths called orbits or shells
• Each orbit has a fixed energy
• Only those orbits are permitted where angular momentum = nh/2π
• Energy is absorbed or emitted only when electron jumps between orbits

2.5 Quantum Mechanical Model

The modern quantum mechanical model describes electrons as probability clouds rather than fixed orbits. It is based on:

• Heisenberg's Uncertainty Principle: Δx × Δp ≥ h/4π
• de Broglie's Equation: λ = h/mv
• Schrödinger's wave equation

2.6 Quantum Numbers

Four quantum numbers completely describe an electron:

• Principal quantum number (n): Determines size and energy of orbit. n = 1, 2, 3...
• Azimuthal quantum number (l): Determines shape. l = 0 to (n-1)
• Magnetic quantum number (mₗ): Determines orientation. mₗ = -l to +l
• Spin quantum number (mₛ): Determines spin. mₛ = +½ or -½

2.7 Electronic Configuration

The distribution of electrons in orbitals follows three rules:

• Aufbau Principle: Electrons fill orbitals in order of increasing energy
• Pauli Exclusion Principle: No two electrons can have same set of quantum numbers
• Hund's Rule: Electrons occupy degenerate orbitals singly first
